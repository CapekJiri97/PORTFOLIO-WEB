Econometric Modeling of Timber Market Dynamics

Timeframe: 2000–2024
Method: TSLS
Source: Czech Statistical Office

About this project:
This project focuses on analyzing the economic and production factors influencing timber harvesting in the Czech Republic (specifically Larch and Pine) between 2000 and 2024. Moving beyond simple linear regression, I developed a Simultaneous Equation Model to capture the interdependent nature of the timber market, providing a robust tool for analyzing market elasticity and predicting future trends.

Tools & Methods
Gretl MS Excel Two-Stage Least Squares (TSLS) VIF Tests Time-Series Analysis

1. Objective & Hypothesis

The goal was to determine which variables significantly affect the volume of Larch logging.

  • Initial Hypothesis: Larch logging is influenced by the logging of other wood types (substitutes or complements), available forest area, and labor force capacity.
  • Advanced Hypothesis: Larch and Pine markets are interconnected; a shock in one market affects the other simultaneously.

2. Data Collection & Preparation

I constructed a dataset using annual time-series data (2000–2024) from the Czech Statistical Office.

  • Endogenous Variables: Larch logging volume, Pine logging volume.
  • Exogenous Variables: Beech logging, Coniferous forest area, Employment in forestry, Annual reforestation.
  • Data Cleaning: Descriptive statistics and correlation matrices were used to detect outliers and potential multicollinearity (verified using VIF tests).

3. Methodology: From OLS to TSLS

Step 1: Single-Equation Model (OLS)

Initially, I used Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) to model Larch logging. While the model showed a high coefficient of determination (R2 ~ 76%), it failed to capture the bidirectional relationship between different wood markets.

Step 2: Simultaneous Equation Model (TSLS)

To address endogeneity, I formulated a system of two structural equations where Larch and Pine logging volumes explain each other.

  • Estimation Method: Two-Stage Least Squares (TSLS).
  • Identification: The Order Condition was checked to ensure the system was solvable.

4. Model Diagnostics

Rigorous econometric verification was performed to ensure the reliability of the estimates:

  • Homoscedasticity: Verified using White’s test and Breusch-Pagan test.
  • Autocorrelation: Checked using Durbin-Watson statistics and Breusch-Godfrey test to ensure independence of residuals.
  • Normality: Residuals were tested for normal distribution to validate statistical inference.

5. Key Findings & Strategic Insights

The simultaneous model revealed crucial market dynamics:

  • Complementarity: A strong positive relationship was found between Larch and Pine logging. An increase in Pine harvesting activity drives Larch harvesting, suggesting shared logistical or market drivers.
  • Elasticity Analysis: I calculated price elasticities to simulate scenarios. For instance, the model predicts that a 1% increase in forestry employment leads to a ~0.18% increase in Larch logging, indicating low labor sensitivity.
  • Forecasting: The model was successfully used to simulate the impact of changes in forest area and labor force on future production volumes.

Personal Reflection

This project enhanced my ability to look beyond simple correlation. By implementing a simultaneous equation model in Gretl, I learned how to handle complex economic systems where variables influence each other in real-time. It demonstrated that advanced statistical methods (TSLS) are essential for accurate modeling in interconnected markets.
